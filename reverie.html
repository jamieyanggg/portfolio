overview

Reverie is a concept mobile app that reframes donating as a calming, daily ritual. Instead of presenting charity as a financial decision, the experience uses soft visuals, emotional reinforcement, and gamified feedback to make giving feel effortless and gratifying.

the challenge

Younger users care deeply about social causes, but rarely donate consistently.

  • ✦

    Users don’t trust where money goes

  • ✦

    Donations feel inconvenient or transactional

  • ✦

    Giving feels small and insignificant

  • ✦

    Most people only donate during emotional moments

my approach

1. User Research

I conducted interviews with 25 people who use meditation apps regularly. The biggest insight? They wanted an app that "gets out of the way" - minimal taps, no decision fatigue, just peace.

2. Visual Language

Developed a gradient-based design system inspired by the transitional moments of day - dawn, dusk, twilight. Each meditation category has its own unique gradient that creates an immediate mood.

3. Interaction Design

Every interaction was designed to feel fluid and gentle. Transitions are slow and smooth, buttons respond with soft haptic feedback, and the interface breathes with subtle animations that mirror breathing patterns.

4. Smart Personalization

Created an adaptive home screen that learns user preferences and suggests the right meditation based on time of day, usage patterns, and mood tracking - without requiring manual input.

what i learned

This project taught me that sometimes the best design is the one users don't notice. Every flashy animation or bold design choice I initially included had to be questioned: "Does this serve the user's need for peace, or does it serve my ego as a designer?"

I learned to design with restraint - to let gradients do the heavy lifting for visual interest, to trust in whitespace, and to make every interaction feel intentional and calming. The most challenging part was convincing stakeholders that "less" could actually be "more premium."

Most importantly, I learned to test with real users in real stress states, not just in controlled environments. Watching someone use the app after a stressful day taught me more than any usability test ever could.
